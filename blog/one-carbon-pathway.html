The Pathway Neurons Use to Protect Themselves — And How We're Harnessing It

A question asked at the University of Cambridge about how neurons survive Alzheimer's disease led to a discovery that sits at the heart of everything OneCarbon does. This is the story of that discovery — and the science behind it.

OneCarbon Science Team

The history of Alzheimer's research is, in large part, a history of looking at what goes wrong. Amyloid plaques. Tau tangles. Neuroinflammation. Mitochondrial failure. Each discovery has deepened our understanding of the disease's pathology — and each, so far, has resisted being turned into an effective treatment. Our research at the University of Cambridge began from a different starting point: rather than asking what kills neurons in Alzheimer's disease, we asked what keeps them alive.

The question

Not all neurons succumb to Alzheimer's disease at the same rate. Even in severely affected brains, some populations of neurons survive far longer than others — maintaining their function, holding their connections, resisting the toxic environment around them. This variability is not random. It reflects real biological differences in how individual neurons respond to the disease's assault.

The question our research set out to answer was: what are the surviving neurons doing that the dying ones are not? If we could identify the molecular mechanisms underlying neuronal resilience in Alzheimer's disease, we might find something worth amplifying — a biological lever that could be pulled to protect more neurons for longer.

The discovery

To answer this question, our team at Cambridge used an AI-driven multiomics approach analysing data across multiple biological levels simultaneously, including the genome, transcriptome, and metabolome of neurons under Alzheimer's-related stress. This kind of analysis, integrating thousands of variables at once, would have been computationally impossible a decade ago. It allowed us to identify patterns that no single experimental approach could have revealed.

What emerged, consistently across multiple datasets and biological levels, was the same signal: neurons that were surviving under Alzheimer's-related stress were upregulating a specific metabolic pathway called one-carbon metabolism. Neurons that were not doing this, or could not sustain it, were dying.

What is one-carbon metabolism?

One-carbon metabolism is a network of interconnected biochemical reactions that transfer single-carbon units between molecules — a process that sounds technical but underlies some of the most fundamental activities a cell can perform. It is the pathway responsible for synthesising and donating the chemical groups that regulate gene expression, produce neurotransmitters, generate antioxidants, and repair DNA.

The pathway takes in folate, B12, and methionine as its primary inputs, and produces molecules essential for gene regulation, antioxidant defence, DNA repair, and neurotransmitter synthesis.

In the context of Alzheimer's disease, three of these outputs are particularly significant. First, one-carbon metabolism produces glutathione, the cell's primary antioxidant — critical for neutralising the oxidative stress that amyloid plaques and tau tangles generate. Second, it supports mitochondrial function directly, helping neurons maintain the energy supply they need to clear toxic proteins and sustain their connections. Third, through a process called methylation, it regulates the expression of genes involved in neuronal survival, effectively tuning the cell's response to stress at the genetic level.

When neurons under Alzheimer's-related stress upregulate one-carbon metabolism, they are not doing something arbitrary. They are activating precisely the mechanisms that address the specific threats the disease poses. This is why the signal appeared so consistently in our data: it is a coherent, targeted biological response.

The human genetic evidence

Finding that stressed neurons upregulate one-carbon metabolism in cell and animal models was compelling, but it raised an obvious question: does this actually matter in humans? To answer this, our team turned to human genetic data, using a technique called Mendelian randomisation to examine whether naturally occurring genetic variants that affect one-carbon metabolism in neurons are associated with Alzheimer's disease risk in the general population.

The findings were consistent with our experimental data. Genetic variants associated with higher expression of one-carbon metabolic genes in neurons were associated with a reduced risk of Alzheimer's disease in large population datasets. This is associative evidence rather than direct causal proof, and should be interpreted accordingly — but it meaningfully strengthens the biological plausibility of the pathway's role in human disease, and suggests that what we observed in the laboratory reflects something real happening in human brains.

From discovery to intervention: building 1C-01

The discovery raised a practical question: if one-carbon metabolism protects neurons, can we enhance it therapeutically? And if so, how?

Direct supplementation with one-carbon metabolites — folate, B12, SAM — is possible, but faces several challenges. Oral supplements are metabolised systemically and do not reliably reach the brain in the concentrations needed to influence neuronal metabolism. Intravenous delivery would be more precise but is impractical as a long-term intervention. What was needed was a delivery mechanism that could produce one-carbon metabolites continuously, at a physiologically relevant site, in a way that was safe and accessible.

The answer was the gut. Gut bacteria are already producers of B vitamins and one-carbon metabolites — it is one of the microbiome's natural roles. And metabolites produced in the gut enter the bloodstream directly, bypassing many of the barriers that limit oral supplementation. The challenge was to engineer a bacterial strain capable of producing the right metabolites at sufficient levels to make a measurable difference.

Using directed evolution — a process of iteratively selecting bacteria for enhanced metabolite production across hundreds of generations — our team developed 1C-01: a probiotic strain optimised to continuously produce key one-carbon metabolites in the gut. In our pilot human study, 1C-01 successfully colonised the gastrointestinal tract, raised one-carbon metabolite levels in the blood, and was well tolerated by participants, several of whom reported subjective improvements in cognitive clarity. These are preliminary findings, and we are cautious about reading too much into self-reported outcomes at this stage.

Where we are now

We are about to launch a larger randomised controlled trial (NCT07457242), led by Prof. Ben Underwood, to rigorously evaluate the clinical efficacy of 1C-01 in a controlled setting. This trial will measure cognitive outcomes, biomarkers of neuronal health, and metabolite profiles over time.

1C-01 is a probiotic and has not been proven to prevent or treat Alzheimer's disease or any other condition. We are committed to building the clinical evidence to the standard the science — and patients — deserve.

Why this approach is different

Most existing and experimental Alzheimer's drugs work by targeting the disease's pathology after it has formed — clearing amyloid, blocking tau aggregation, dampening neuroinflammation. These are valid strategies, but they face a fundamental timing problem: by the time the pathology is detectable, significant neuronal loss has already occurred. The damage they are trying to reverse may be, in large part, irreversible.

OneCarbon's approach is upstream of that damage. One-carbon metabolism is activated by neurons in response to stress — meaning it is engaged during the long preclinical window, before neuronal death, before cognitive symptoms, before the therapeutic window has narrowed. By supporting that response continuously via the gut, 1C-01 is designed to act precisely when protection matters most: early, quietly, and without the side effect profile of drugs that cross the blood-brain barrier.

There is more work to do. The clinical trial will determine whether the biological rationale translates into measurable benefit for patients. But the question we started with — how do neurons protect themselves from Alzheimer's disease? — has led somewhere genuinely new.
