What is One-Carbon Metabolism?

April 2026 · Science

In this article

  1. Why it matters for the brain
  2. What disrupts it?
  3. Our approach

One-carbon metabolism is one of the most important — and least talked about — metabolic networks in the human body. It gets its name from the single-carbon units (methyl groups) that are transferred between molecules throughout the cycle. These transfers power an enormous range of cellular processes: DNA methylation, amino acid synthesis, redox balance, and mitochondrial energy production.

Why it matters for the brain

In brief: Neurons depend on one-carbon metabolism to maintain key methyl donors and support mitochondrial function — and in Alzheimer's disease, this pathway is significantly impaired, linking one-carbon metabolism to core disease hallmarks.

Neurons are among the most metabolically demanding cells in the body. They rely on one-carbon metabolism to maintain key methyl donors that are critical for neurotransmitter synthesis and for keeping the epigenetic marks that regulate gene expression in order.

In Alzheimer's disease, this pathway is significantly impaired. Multiple hallmarks of the disease — amyloid-β accumulation, tau pathology, and oxidative damage — converge on mitochondrial dysfunction, which is itself directly linked to disrupted one-carbon metabolism.

What disrupts it?

In brief: Nutrient deficiencies, genetic variants, and ageing can all impair one-carbon metabolism — with elevated homocysteine serving as a measurable marker of dysfunction independently linked to dementia risk.

The cycle can be disrupted by nutrient deficiencies (particularly folate and B12), genetic variants, and the normal process of ageing. Elevated homocysteine — a well-known cardiovascular risk marker — is a direct consequence of one-carbon cycle dysfunction and has been independently associated with dementia risk.
