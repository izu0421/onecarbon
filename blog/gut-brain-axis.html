The Gut–Brain Axis and Neurodegeneration

January 2026 · Science

In this article

  1. What the evidence shows
  2. Where one-carbon metabolism fits in

The idea that the gut and the brain are in constant communication — influencing each other through hormones, the immune system, and the vagus nerve — has gone from fringe hypothesis to mainstream neuroscience in under a decade. The gut–brain axis is now one of the most active areas of research in both neurology and microbiology.

What the evidence shows

In brief: Animal and human studies link gut microbiome composition to Alzheimer's and Parkinson's disease, with patients showing reduced microbial diversity and altered metabolite profiles. Several mechanisms — including gut-driven inflammation and effects on the blood-brain barrier — are under active investigation.

Studies in both animal models and human populations have found correlations between gut microbiome composition and neurodegenerative conditions including Alzheimer's and Parkinson's disease. Patients with Alzheimer's consistently show reduced microbial diversity and an altered metabolite profile compared to age-matched controls.

Whether this is cause or consequence — or both — is still being investigated. But several mechanisms have been proposed: systemic inflammation driven by gut dysbiosis; altered production of short-chain fatty acids that affect the blood-brain barrier; and direct effects on neurotransmitter precursors.

Where one-carbon metabolism fits in

In brief: The gut microbiome plays an important role in producing metabolites that support neuronal health, providing the biological rationale for 1C-01 — a probiotic designed to support the metabolic cycle that neurons depend on for protection.
