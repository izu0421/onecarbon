What is One-Carbon Metabolism?

April 2026 · Science

One-carbon metabolism is one of the most important — and least talked about — metabolic networks in the human body. It gets its name from the single-carbon units (methyl groups) that are transferred between molecules throughout the cycle. These transfers power an enormous range of cellular processes: DNA methylation, amino acid synthesis, redox balance, and mitochondrial energy production.

Why it matters for the brain

Neurons are among the most metabolically demanding cells in the body. They rely on one-carbon metabolism to maintain the supply of SAM (S-adenosylmethionine) — the brain's primary methyl donor. SAM is critical for neurotransmitter synthesis and for keeping the epigenetic marks that regulate gene expression in order.

In Alzheimer's disease, SAM levels in the brain are significantly depleted. Multiple hallmarks of the disease — amyloid-β accumulation, tau pathology, and oxidative damage — converge on mitochondrial dysfunction, which is itself directly linked to impaired one-carbon metabolism.

What disrupts it?

The cycle can be disrupted by nutrient deficiencies (particularly folate and B12), genetic variants, and the normal process of ageing. Elevated homocysteine — a well-known cardiovascular risk marker — is a direct consequence of one-carbon cycle dysfunction and has been independently associated with dementia risk.

Our approach

At OneCarbon, we discovered that certain gut bacteria naturally produce one-carbon metabolites that can support this cycle. Using directed evolution, we developed 1C-01 — a probiotic strain that continuously produces these metabolites in the gut, providing a sustained and natural route to supporting one-carbon metabolism.
